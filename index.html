Welcome

Floods are among the most frequent and destructive natural disasters in Sri Lanka, affecting thousands of lives and causing severe economic and infrastructural damage annually. The Gampaha District, situated in the wet zone of the country, is particularly vulnerable due to its dense population, rapid urbanization, and exposure to both the southwest and northeast monsoons. One of the most flood-prone areas within this district is the Attanagalu Oya river basin, which frequently overflows during intense rainfall, inundating low-lying Grama Niladhari (GN) divisions.
In response to these recurring threats, this research project aims to develop a machine learning (ML)-based flood area prediction model that leverages local environmental and hydrological data. By integrating features such as river water levels, rainfall intensity, and the distance of GN divisions from the river, the model classifies areas as either flood-prone or not. The system is designed not only to enhance the accuracy and timeliness of early flood warnings but also to support better disaster preparedness, resource allocation, and community resilience planning.

About the Project

Attanagalu Oya Basin spans 727 km² and frequently floods due to high rainfall and inadequate drainage. This project aims to model flood-prone zones using historical data from 2016 onward, sourced from the Irrigation Department, DMC, and Meteorology Department. Our goal is to develop an accurate and responsive ML-based system to help authorities anticipate risks and minimize impacts.

Methodology

The data preparation involved the following stages:

  • Collection of water level, rainfall, and flood event records from 2016 to present
  • Digitizing paper records and standardizing timestamps
  • Cleaning and merging datasets using Python (pandas)
  • Engineering features including distance from river and risk categorization
  • Aligning flood events with corresponding hydrological measurements

We trained three machine learning models — SVM, Random Forest, and XGBoost — to classify each GN division as flood-prone (1) or not (0). These models were evaluated using standard classification metrics to identify the most accurate one for deployment.

Results

Initial testing shows that Random Forest and XGBoost provided the highest accuracy in model evaluation.

Model performance was evaluated using confusion matrix, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC curves. Once testing is complete, the most accurate model will be selected for integration into a real-time flood early warning system.

Discussion

ML models like Random Forest and XGBoost outperform traditional static models in capturing flood risk due to their ability to learn from non-linear and multi-variable relationships. The inclusion of spatial features (distance to river) and hydrological data enhances prediction precision. Future plans involve integration of sensor networks and real-time alerts.
